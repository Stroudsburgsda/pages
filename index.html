Sabbath School Momentum

Lesson for Sabbath 5/23/2026

When Faith Feels Fragile

It’s easy to have faith when life is going well. However, when prayers seem unanswered, doubts creep in, and circumstances don’t make sense, our faith can feel fragile. In this week’s lesson, we are reminded that faith is “the reality of what we hope for” and “the evidence of things we cannot see.” However, strong faith does not mean the absence of struggle, questions, or even moments of unbelief.

One of the most powerful reminders from this week’s study is that faith is not a feeling. Feelings fluctuate, and circumstances shift. Faith chooses to trust God’s character even when clarity is limited and emotions are unsettled.

Hebrews 11 Reflection

In Hebrews 11, we find a beautiful portrait of ordinary people who anchored their lives in God’s promises. They did not see everything fulfilled in their lifetime, yet they believed, obeyed, and persevered. Their stories remind us that faith is not built on what we can control or fully understand; it is built on the One who is faithful.

The invitation of this week’s lesson is not to attempt to manufacture stronger faith through our effort, but to cultivate a deeper relationship with Christ, who is the Author and Finisher of our faith. As you study this week, engage with the questions, revisit the stories, and explore what it truly means to live by faith.

Question of the Week

What is the relationship between “feeling” and “faith” in the Christian experience?

A. The feeling of peace is the primary indicator that a person has exercised faith.
B. If a person lacks joyful feelings, it is a definitive sign that they do not have a saving measure of faith.
C. Faith and feeling are identical in the Greek text of the New Testament.
D. Faith is a human response prompted by the Spirit, whereas joyful feeling is a blessing that God may choose to give as a result of faith.
Correct Answer: D
Food for Thought:

When your faith is tested, what most shapes your response: your feelings, your circumstances, past experiences, or your understanding of God’s character? How can Scripture help strengthen your trust?

Religious Liberty

Rededicate 250: Revival or Something More?

The recent Rededicate 250 gathering in Washington D.C. featured repeated calls for miracles, revival, fire, healing, power, deliverance, and a new beginning for America. While prayer and repentance are needed in our nation, an important question must be asked: What kind of revival is being promoted? From a biblical perspective, true revival is not simply emotional worship, public enthusiasm, or national unity. Genuine revival always leads people back to deeper obedience to God, His Word, and His truth.

Jesus repeatedly warned that one of the major signs of the last days would be spiritual deception. “Take heed that no man deceive you” (Matthew 24:4), and “For there shall arise false Christs, and false prophets, and shall shew great signs and wonders” (Matthew 24:24). Christ warned that powerful religious movements would arise appearing spiritual and persuasive, yet capable of leading many away from truth. Scripture teaches that supernatural manifestations alone are never proof of God’s approval.

The Great Controversy Reality

The Great Controversy also warns that popular revivals can rely heavily on emotion and excitement while neglecting important biblical truths. “Popular revivals are too often carried by appeals to the imagination, by exciting the emotions…” (The Great Controversy, p. 463). The issue is not prayer itself or even unity itself. The concern arises when emotional experiences become more important than Scripture and when movements seek broad agreement while avoiding truths that require self-denial, obedience, and commitment to all of God’s Word.

Bible prophecy warns of a coming religious movement that will influence the world through authority, worship, and even miraculous signs. Revelation 13:14 says that deception will occur “by the means of those miracles which he had power to do.” As Seventh-day Adventists, we understand that the final issue centers on worship and allegiance to God. The concern is not merely religious gatherings, but the possibility of increasing religious influence joining with civil power in ways that eventually place pressure on conscience.

As believers, we are called not to respond with fear, but with discernment. True revival will never lead us away from Scripture or minimize God’s truth in exchange for unity and emotional experience. Genuine revival points people to Jesus Christ, faithfulness to His commandments, and complete surrender to His will. Now more than ever, God’s people must test every movement by His Word: “To the law and to the testimony” (Isaiah 8:20).
