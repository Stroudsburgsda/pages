When the City Is Messy, but God Is Still Moving

New quarter. New lessons. New opportunities to let God shape us.

This week, we explore Corinth, a city buzzing with commerce, ambition, immorality, and spiritual confusion. It wasn’t the kind of place you would expect revival to break out. Yet that’s exactly where God sent Paul.

Sometimes we think God works best in ideal conditions; you know, when life is calm, our church is united, and our faith feels strong. But Paul’s ministry in Corinth reminds us that God often does His deepest work in the messiest places.

Paul arrives in Corinth after what seemed like a difficult season. Athens had been intellectually challenging. Opposition followed him. Fear lingered. In fact, Paul later admitted he came “in weakness, in fear, and in much trembling.”

Met in Weakness

“Do not be afraid… for I am with you.”

Maybe you’re entering this new quarter tired. Spiritually stretched. Carrying unanswered prayers, personal battles, or ministry fatigue. But notice this: God didn’t wait for Paul to feel strong before using him. Instead, God met him in his weakness.

That’s the heartbeat of this week’s lesson. Your calling still matters. Paul knew who he was, he was called by God. Not because of his perfection, but because of God’s purpose. And the same is true for us.

You may not be an apostle, but you are called:
  • Called to influence your family
  • Called to serve your church
  • Called to reflect Christ in your workplace
  • Called to disciple someone

Purpose isn’t reserved for preachers. It belongs to every believer. As we journey through Corinthians this quarter, we will wrestle with real church issues: division, immaturity, morality, conflict, and grace. And maybe that’s why this study matters so much, because it reminds us that God has always worked through imperfect people in imperfect communities.

So don’t just read the lesson this quarter. Engage with it. Wrestle with it. Pray through it. Let it read you. Because somewhere in the chaos of Corinth, we may just find ourselves.

Food For Thought

"If God could build a thriving church in Corinth, what might He be trying to build in the “Corinth” areas of your life right now?"

The Mission Before the Message

Pastoral Heart Prep for July 4th

On July 4, many of us will walk through the streets of Stroudsburg wearing our church shirts, carrying gift bags, and inviting people to upcoming events. For some, that will feel natural. For others, it may feel uncomfortable.

The truth is that most people are not afraid of talking about Jesus. They are afraid of being rejected. What if someone says no? What if they ignore me? What if I don’t know what to say?

These are normal concerns, but they remind us of an important truth: our job is not to convince anyone. Our job is simply to make ourselves available for God to use.

Divine Sowers

Jesus understood these fears, yet He still sent His disciples into towns and villages with a simple mission: go, serve, and trust God with the results. Every follower of Christ is called to sow seeds. God alone brings the harvest.

The person standing in front of you may have had a difficult week. They may be carrying burdens no one else knows about. They may be lonely, discouraged, grieving, or searching for answers. We often assume people are not interested when, in reality, many are simply waiting for someone to show genuine kindness.

As we prepare for outreach, remember that we are not representing ourselves. We are ambassadors for Christ. Every smile, every conversation, and every act of kindness gives someone an opportunity to catch a glimpse of His character.

Five Simple Principles to Remember

1

Start with a Smile, Not a Speech

You do not need a rehearsed presentation. A smile, a greeting, and a friendly attitude often open more doors than a lengthy introduction. People respond to warmth and sincerity.

2

Offer the Gift Bag First

The bag is simply a way to bless our neighbors. It contains resources, an invitation, and a reminder that our church is here to serve the community. We are giving because Christ first gave to us.

"We’re from the Stroudsburg Seventh-day Adventist Church and we’re sharing a free gift with our community today."

The goal is to serve first and allow curiosity to follow.

3

Let the T-Shirt Start the Conversation

Our shirts were designed to create curiosity. If someone comments on your shirt or asks a question, that becomes a natural opportunity to share why we are there and what our church is doing in the community. If someone comments, simply explain that we are sharing hope, prayer, and information about upcoming community events.

4

Offer Prayer Naturally

One of the greatest gifts we can offer is prayer. A simple question such as, “Is there anything we can pray about for you?” may open the door to a conversation God has already been preparing. Some may share a burden. Others may simply thank you. Either way, they will know someone cared enough to ask.

Sometimes the greatest ministry is not having all the answers, but simply being willing to listen.

5

Leave Them with an Invitation, Not Pressure

Invite them to End Game. Let them know they are always welcome. Then trust God with the results. The Holy Spirit is responsible for changing hearts. We are responsible for being faithful.

Before approaching anyone, take a silent moment to pray. Ask God to lead you to the person who most needs encouragement that day. Rather than worrying about what you will say, trust that the Holy Spirit will guide both your words and your steps. Sometimes the divine appointments we pray for begin with something as simple as, “Good morning.”

Not every conversation will be long. Not every person will stop. Not every flyer will be read immediately. But every encounter is an opportunity to reflect the character of Christ.

If they decline

Wish them a happy Fourth of July and move on graciously.

If they accept

Thank them for their time and bless them.

If they share a burden

Listen carefully and offer prayer on the spot.

Success is not measured by how many people stop. Success is measured by whether we represent Jesus well. This outreach is not ultimately about distributing literature. It is about making connections. The flyer may be remembered. The gift bag may be appreciated. But often what people remember most is how they were treated.

So before we hand out a single flyer, let us ask God to give us His eyes, His compassion, and His courage. The Holy Spirit has already gone before us, preparing hearts that we cannot see. Our responsibility is simply to show up, be faithful, and allow God to work through ordinary conversations.

We may never know this side of heaven what one smile, one prayer, or one invitation will accomplish. But God does. Let’s go into our community with confidence, knowing that every act of kindness done in Christ’s name has eternal value.
