Restoration Hour of Prayer (Upcoming Quarter)

We have a powerful and deeply meaningful quarter ahead for our Women’s Ministries department during the Restoration Hour of Prayer. Over the next few months, they will continue covering the vital topic of "Church Hurt."

Understanding the Reality

“Church hurt” refers to the emotional, psychological, or spiritual harm inflicted by church leaders or fellow believers. It can leave deep wounds, but recognizing your pain is valid is the first step toward healing.

Sources of Church Hurt:
  • Misuse of Spiritual Authority: Leaders using scripture or spiritual language to control, shame, or exploit others.
  • Relational Harm: Gossip, betrayal, exclusion, or failure of the church to support you in a crisis.
  • Toxic Church Culture: Prioritizing numerical growth over people or allowing immature/unhealthy patterns to go unchecked.
Steps for Healing:
  • Grieve & Detangle: Allow yourself to feel the pain. It is important to separate your personal faith and view of God from the imperfect, fallible humans who caused your pain.
  • Set Boundaries: You have the right to step away from a toxic environment or take a break from attending services to protect your mental health.
  • Seek Support: Healing is rarely a solo journey. Speaking with a licensed mental health professional can help you process religious trauma or deep-seated emotional wounds.

Stewardship Spotlight

Theme: Count the Cost

“For which of you, intending to build a tower, does not sit down first and count the cost…?”

— Luke 14:28

What does it mean to “count the cost”?

It means to be intentional. Before we commit, before we spend, before we build—we pause, we plan, and we seek understanding. In our financial lives, this looks like:

  • Not rushing into decisions
  • Not spending impulsively
  • Not committing beyond what we can sustain

Instead, it means asking: Can I afford this? Is this aligned with my priorities? Have I invited God into this decision?

A Stewardship Perspective

Being a good steward means we don’t move—big or small—without speaking to God first, counting the cost, and building it into our spending plan. Impulse decisions often lead to stress, strain, and misalignment. Intentional decisions lead to peace, clarity, and alignment with God’s will.

A Word for All Generations

  • To our youth: learning to pause before you spend is a powerful habit that will serve you for life.
  • To our young adults and families: planning ahead brings order to your finances and helps you avoid unnecessary pressure.
  • To our Young At Heart: your wisdom and experience remind us all of the value of patience, planning, and trusting God’s timing.
  • And to all: you are never too young or too old to develop new habits and change old ones.

Our Encouragement This Week:

Take time to reflect on this scripture. Before your next financial decision—big or small: Pause. Pray. Plan. Invite God into your decisions and count the cost.

Because stewardship is not just about managing money—it’s about honoring God with every decision we make. May God richly bless your faithfulness as you walk in wisdom and intention.

Sabbath School Momentum

Lesson for Sabbath 5/30/2026

When Sin Isn’t Taken Seriously

What happens when we stop calling sin… sin?

Sin is often softened, renamed, excused, or normalized. We call it weakness, preference, personality, or simply “being human.” But this week’s lesson, “Sin, the Gospel, and the Law,” challenges us to look at sin through God’s eyes; not to condemn us, but to awaken us.

The Bible presents sin as more than isolated mistakes or bad habits. Sin damages our relationship with God, distorts our relationships with others, and quietly reshapes our hearts. The lesson doesn’t leave us trapped in guilt; rather, it points us to the purpose of God’s law and the hope of the gospel. God’s law acts like a mirror; it reveals what we might otherwise ignore. It exposes the reality of sin and reflects God’s character of love and righteousness. However, the law was never meant to save us.

The Hope of the Gospel

The gospel reminds us that our standing before God does not rest on flawless law-keeping, self-improvement, or religious performance. Our hope rests in Christ’s righteousness, His life, His sacrifice, His victory on our behalf. From that place of grace, obedience becomes a response of love, not a burden to earn salvation.

This week’s lesson invites us into an honest conversation about sin, surrender, law, grace, and what it truly means to know God, not just intellectually, but relationally and transformationally. Study deeply this week. Reflect honestly. And allow Scripture to challenge both your understanding and your walk with Christ.

Question Matrix

Food for Thought (for Sabbath discussion):

How can we take sin seriously, uphold the importance of God’s law, and live so rooted in the gospel that our obedience flows from love rather than fear, guilt, or legalism?

Religious Liberty

Rome, Unity, and the Growing Push for Social Order

Two major developments this week highlight the increasing global influence of Rome in both religious and social affairs. First, the U.S. Catholic bishops announced plans to consecrate America to the “Sacred Heart of Jesus” on June 11, 2026, as part of the nation’s 250th anniversary observance. At the same time, Pope Leo XIV continues advancing ecumenical efforts aimed at achieving “full unity” with the Orthodox churches. These initiatives are being presented as movements for healing, peace, reconciliation, and moral renewal, yet they also reveal Rome’s growing effort to strengthen its spiritual and moral influence on a global scale.

According to the Catholic Herald article, the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops will lead a national consecration during its Spring Plenary Assembly in Orlando, Florida. Catholics nationwide are being encouraged to participate through Eucharistic adoration, works of mercy, and prayer leading up to the event. Meanwhile, Vatican News reported Pope Leo XIV’s renewed call for stronger ecumenical commitment and “full unity” with the Orthodox churches, emphasizing that such unity is urgently needed in a world marked by conflict and division.

Prophetic Perspective

From a prophetic perspective, these developments are significant because Scripture warns that the Papacy’s influence would increase again in the last days. Revelation 13 describes a power whose “deadly wound was healed: and all the world wondered after the beast” (Revelation 13:3). Seventh-day Adventists have long understood this to point toward a restoration of global religious influence centered in Rome. The concern is not merely that churches seek cooperation, but that increasing religious unity combined with moral and political influence may eventually place pressure upon conscience and blur the line between church and state.

At the same time, Pope Leo XIV released a new encyclical titled Magnifica Humanitas, addressing labor, economics, government, digital regulation, and the “common good.” The document repeatedly builds upon Rerum Novarum and the teachings of Pope Leo XIII, who openly advocated for government protection of Sunday rest. While the new encyclical never explicitly mentions Sunday observance, it strongly emphasizes the role of governments in ensuring proper balance between “work, leisure, and rest” and calls for greater cooperation between civil authorities and Catholic social teaching.

The encyclical also advocates stronger oversight of digital platforms, economic systems, and global governance structures. Pope Leo XIV warns against allowing major technological and economic powers to operate without increased regulation and public oversight. These appeals are being framed around fairness, unity, worker protection, and social stability. Yet from a prophetic viewpoint, increasing centralized control over economics, communication, and social order raises important questions about liberty of conscience and the future ability of dissenting voices to freely operate.

As Seventh-day Adventists, we recognize that the final crisis described in Revelation centers on worship and allegiance to God. The issue is not whether rest, unity, or social order are valuable. The issue is who ultimately defines truth and whether religious influence will increasingly shape civil law and public policy. History repeatedly demonstrates that when church and state unite for the “common good,” individual liberty of conscience often suffers.

The Great Controversy warned of this very development: “She is silently growing into power. Her doctrines are exerting their influence in legislative halls, in the churches, and in the hearts of men” (The Great Controversy, p. 581). While these events do not themselves constitute the final fulfillment of prophecy, they do reveal the growing conditions Scripture warned would exist before earth’s closing scenes. Now more than ever, God’s people must remain grounded in Scripture, faithful to Christ, and spiritually discerning. The world is increasingly searching for moral leadership, unity, and solutions to global instability. As these movements continue to grow, we must carefully test every message, every movement, and every call for unity by the Word of God.
