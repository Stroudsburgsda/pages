Sabbath School Momentum

Lesson for Sabbath 5/16/2026

A Prayer Life That Goes Beyond Routine

For the second week in a row, the lesson focuses on prayer. This week’s lesson, “Practical Prayer,” invites us to rethink prayer. Prayer is not a religious obligation, but it is what sustains a vibrant, daily connection with God. Prayer is meant to be more than a quick request before meals or a desperate cry during difficult moments.

As we study Scripture, we see that some of the most powerful moments happened through honest, heartfelt prayer. Hannah prayed through her heartbreak. Elijah prayed in exhaustion and fear. Daniel prayed with humility, confession, and hope. Their prayers were not polished performances; rather, they were real conversations with a God who listens. God is not waiting for perfect words; He is seeking sincere hearts.

Simplicity & Surrender

In the Lord’s Prayer, Jesus Himself taught that prayer is not about impressing others with eloquent language or lengthy speeches. Instead, He modeled simplicity, authenticity, surrender, and trust. Prayer becomes transformative when it moves beyond repetition and becomes a relationship.

How do we handle prayers that seem to be unanswered? Do we trust God only when the answers come quickly and clearly? Or can we continue praying in seasons of waiting, silence, or uncertainty? Sometimes God answers us dramatically, like fire from heaven on Mount Carmel. Other times, He speaks through the “still small voice” that requires us to slow down and listen. Prayer calls us to pause to pour out our hearts before God. So this week’s lesson is more than a study of prayer techniques. It is an invitation to deepen our communion with Christ, strengthen our trust in God’s timing, and cultivate a prayer life that is practical, personal, and persistent.

Stewardship Spotlight

Count the Cost

This week, we turn our attention to a powerful question found in Gospel of Luke:

“For which of you, intending to build a tower, does not sit down first and count the cost…?”

— Luke 14:28

What does it mean to “count the cost”?

It means to be intentional. Before we commit, before we spend, before we build—we pause, we plan, and we seek understanding. In our financial lives, this looks like:

  • Not rushing into decisions
  • Not spending impulsively
  • Not committing beyond what we can sustain

Instead, it means asking: Can I afford this? Is this aligned with my priorities? Have I invited God into this decision?

A Stewardship Perspective

Being a good steward means we don’t move—big or small—without speaking to God first, counting the cost, and building it into our spending plan. Impulse decisions often lead to stress, strain, and misalignment. Intentional decisions lead to peace, clarity, and alignment with God’s will.

A Word for All Generations

  • To our youth: learning to pause before you spend is a powerful habit that will serve you for life.
  • To our young adults and families: planning ahead brings order to your finances and helps you avoid unnecessary pressure.
  • To our Young At Heart: your wisdom and experience remind us all of the value of patience, planning, and trusting God’s timing.
  • And to all: you are never too young or too old to develop new habits and change old ones.

Our Encouragement This Week:

Take time to reflect on this scripture. Before your next financial decision—big or small: **Pause. Pray. Plan.** Invite God into your decisions and count the cost.

Stewardship is about honoring God with every decision.

Religious Liberty

Preparing to Stand

When National Worship Becomes a Public Cause

This week, President Donald Trump issued a proclamation recognizing a national day of prayer specifically connected to the Jewish community and the importance of faith in America’s identity. While many view this as a positive gesture of solidarity and religious support, it also highlights an important prophetic principle: once government leaders begin publicly recognizing, promoting, or setting aside specific days connected to worship and religious observance, a precedent is established.

The Prophetic Principle

If leaders can call for recognition surrounding one faith community’s sacred observance, it becomes easier for future calls to be made around broader Christian worship practices, including Sunday observance. In times of crisis, economic instability, natural disasters, or social unrest, calls for national prayer and Sunday rest laws could increasingly be framed as solutions for moral restoration and national unity.

The Bible warns that the final conflict will center on worship and allegiance. Revelation 13 describes a union of religious influence and civil power that ultimately pressures conscience. Revelation 14 calls God’s people back to worship the Creator according to His Word. The issue at the end of time is not merely politics. It is worship. Who has authority over conscience: God or man?

As God’s people, we must respond with discernment, not fear. Now is the time to know why we believe what we believe. A faith that stands in the final moments will not be built on emotion, culture, or popular opinion, but on a deep personal conviction rooted in the Word of God.

Important Reminders

  • Government recognition establishes precedent
  • Crises increase calls for religious unity
  • The final issue centers on worship and conscience
